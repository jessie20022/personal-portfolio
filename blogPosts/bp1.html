semester 3 of college, semester 1 of in-person education; studying for CS?

December 27, 2021

I feel speechless when reflecting on this past semester. On one hand, I'm glad I've met so many new people that I love spending time with. On the other hand, I feel like I have to change my study habits and I still have so much more to learn. I've always been someone who loved school, and online school was the best for me. While many of my friends would often complain about how they loathed taking classes online, I thrived. I found it difficult to go back in person: having to walk 15 minutes to my classes and socializing daily was not something I was excited for or used to.

I'd say adjusting was to being in-person again was one of the greatest changes. It's something everyone had to do. Socially, it wasn't too difficult. I definitely found myself much less social than before COVID started, but I think it was better for my studies. In terms of school, I realized how I needed to study differently. For my math classes, I've used the same study habits since high school and found them very effective. However, I found flaws in how I studied for computer science.

Everyone's different, but I'll discuss my realizations. Whether this proves helpful for others is entirely subjective. Starting off college, computer science was entirely online. This makes sense, considering lecture halls contained over 150 students. Our exams were always mostly coding a program of multiple classes to follow a prompt with roughly 20 multiple choice added on. I studied well from watching lectures, completing projects, and throughly understanding lecture code (but not working with it).

Studying for 216 wasn't the same as studying for 131 or 132. Provided for were old practice exams. They were a few pages, half consisting of code, the other half being a mixture of writing short linux commands and multiple choice. All of the students went into the exam thinking it'd be the same, since the previous exams of almost 10 years followed the same format.

The actual exam was nothing like any of us had thought. 80% of the exam was writing your own code and the other 20% was a mixture of linux commands and identifying the problem in given code. Sure, any amazing computer sicentist would be able to adapt to this unexpected format, but most of the class was unprepared. And theortically, since I performed well in my first-year CS classes in coding, I should have been able to perform well on most of this exam. However, coming out of the lecture hall, everyone had fearful expressions on their faces. I think we all realized that studying from previous exams was entirely useless.

So did I change my study habits for exam 2? Nope. (HAHA) I still studied from the previous exams in hopes that they'd be useful, but I was wrong. On the first exam, I managed to perform above average. However, on exam 2, I performed far below average. I was quite disappointed, but I didn't really have to fear about my score since the grading takes 2 months to be completed (literally). Our professor revealed to study by compiling and altering the provided lecture code. So that's exactly what I did for the final exam.

We just received our final grades and our exam grades on the final. The average was a 49/100. I performed above average, but barely. I studied as the professor told us to: by reviewing, editing, and breaking the lecture code. I suppose I performed better than the second exam, but not better than the first (I was closer to to the average). I believe that if I hadn't studied the way the instructor said, I would have performed worse; but it could have been luck as well since my study buddy didn't perform as well. I don't think there's a uniform way that all CS classes should be studied for.

That being said, what did I learn? Well, I learned a lot about C, Assembly, and computer systems. However, I still have no idea how to study for computer science. My experience is entirely subjective: many may not agree with what I have to say and many may have had a worse or better experience. Taking CMSC216 was the first class in a long time in which I knew I would take the L in. I think it's been difficult trying to figure out the best way to study for this class, and I don't think that there's one way to study for all CS classes. In other CS classes, studying past CS exams might help since you'd have an idea of the exam's structure. However, this class proved not.

Another lesson I learned was not to expect the unexpected. Another cliche phrase, I know. It's something my mom would always say to me growing up, but I didn't use it when I should have this semester. I need to prepare for anything to happen, which can be done by studying more. So next semester, I'll study past exams, code samples, and lecture code.
