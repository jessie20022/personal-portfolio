a recap...

The following section is dedicated to the college courses I've taken at UMD! I tend to get false impressions or hear different things from other people about these classes, but I want to record and reflect on my own experience. It's mostly for personal recollection, sort of like a school journal, but feel free to read on! Courses will be listed in order by semesters and course name. The number in parenthesis represents the number of credits the course is.

Fall 2020

  • CMSC131: Object-Oriented Programming I

    • Course Description: Introduction to programming and computer science. Emphasizes understanding and implementation of applications using object-oriented techniques. Develops skills such as program design and testing as well as implementation of programs using a graphical IDE. Programming done in Java.

    Reflection: One of my more relaxing core classes, since I had prior programming experience. The projects were really fun to think about too, since they were mostly like logic puzles.

  • HNUH100: Gateway Seminar

    • Course Description: First-semester orientation and exploration seminar required of all UH students.

    Reflection: We read Weapons of Math Destruction sorta like a book club. We also explored ancient text/maps, and it was quite interesting. I also became friends with a really cool guy.

  • HNUH248Y: How Do You "Man Up?": Men, Masculinity, and Mental Health

    • Course Description: Students in this interdisciplinary course will explore multi-layered issues related to privilege and oppression through their own life experiences via exposure to theory, research, film, memoirs, and current events. Students will evaluate and critique common assumptions about the meaning and experiences of privilege and oppression using Intersectionality theory as a guiding framework. The human experience related to various social identities (i.e., race, gender and gender identity, sexual orientation, social class, religion, age, and ability) will be addressed.

    Reflection: Super fun and interesting with the help of the professor. One of the more interesting units was probably learning about the stereotypes.

  • MATH240: Introduction to Linear Algebra

    • Course Description: Basic concepts of linear algebra: vector spaces, applications to line and plane geometry, linear equations and matrices, similar matrices, linear transformations, eigenvalues, determinants and quadratic forms.

    Reflection: Really enjoyed the eigenvalue/eigenvector chapter; solving those problems were fun. I also liked the beginning where we learned matrices. It became difficult when we started learning about transformations though :( definitely half-assed and need to review that unit.

  • PHYS121: Fundamentals of Phyics I

    • Course Description: The first part of a two-semester course in general physics treating the fields of mechanics, heat, sound, electricity, magnetism, optics, and modern physics. Together with PHYS122, this generally satisfies the minimum requirement of medical and dental schools.

    Reflection: I took it because I considered going pre-med? Which... that idea didn't age quite well haha. Anyhow, it was basically Physics 1 so it wasn't too bad. I learned most of the material in high school, so the course was basically a review. The downcurve stressed me out, but it was okay! Also met and befriended one of the best people in that class, and he was my lab partner.

Winter 2021

  • INAG110: Oral Communication

    • Course Description: A study of how perception, self-concept, and verbal and nonverbal communications affect the communication process as it emerges in the workplace. The course provides skill training in speech writing, public speaking, group communication, interpersonal communication, listening, and responding.

    Reflection: I had a lot of fun: I got to do 3/4 presentations on topics I was very familiar with. It was stressful having to do four presentations in 3 weeks, not to mention taking a midterm and final. Since I took the class online, it was a bit less nervewrecking presenting over a Zoom than in real life. However, I did learn all about Zoom etiquette and how to properly be interviewed online.

Spring 2021

  • BSCI170: Principles of Molecular & Cellular Biology (3)

    • Course Description: Basic principles of biology with special emphasis on cellular and molecular biology.

    Reflection: I took bio and bio lab so I could take more neuroscience classes in the future, but this class was a great first step. Turns out I knew some of the fundamentals from high school biology, but I still definitely learned a lot of new material such as the details of photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

  • BSCI171: Principles of Molecular & Cellular Biology Laboratory (1)

    • Course Description: Basic laboratory principles of biology with special emphasis on cellular and molecular biology.

    Reflection: Do I think that the expected time spent on this 1 credit class should be 10 hours per week is absurd? Yes. Otherwise, make the class more credits plz :) In all realness, the multi-week labs became incredibly difficult whereas the first five one-week labs weren't too strenous (in fact they were more so busy work in comparison).

  • CMSC132: Object-Oriented Programming II (4)

    • Course Description: Introduction to use of computers to solve problems using software engineering principles. Design, build, test, and debug medium -size software systems and learn to use relevant tools. Use object-oriented methods to create effective and efficient problem solutions. Use and implement application programming interfaces (APIs). Programming done in Java.

    Reflection: Learning all of the data structures was definitely a challenge. I think ArrayLists and Hash/Tree sets are my favorites, but I've gotta be more familiarized with binary search trees. Linked lists aren't bad too, but it's in the same boat as binary search trees.

  • CMSC250: Discrete Structures (4)

    • Course Description: Fundamental mathematical concepts related to computer science, including finite and infinite sets, relations, functions, and propositional logic. Introduction to other techniques, modeling and solving problems in computer science. Introduction to permutations, combinations, graphs, and trees with selected applications.

    Reflection: One of my top three classes at UMD as of May 2021. As I write this, my final grade hasn't come out. The class was definitely a challenge for many including myself, but I loved how much I learned and how the class was taught with lectures T/TH and weekly homeworks.

  • HNUH248A: Identity, Places, & Spaces (3)

    • Course Description: Students in this interdisciplinary course will explore multi-layered issues related to privilege and oppression through their own life experiences via exposure to theory, research, film, memoirs, and current events. Students will evaluate and critique common assumptions about the meaning and experiences of privilege and oppression using Intersectionality theory as a guiding framework. The human experience related to various social identities (i.e., race, gender and gender identity, sexual orientation, social class, religion, age, and ability) will be addressed.

    Reflection: "My eyes have been opened" is the saying I'd use to describe what this class has done for me.

  • MUSC140: Music Fundamentals I (3)

    • Course Description: Introductory theory course. Notation, scales, intervals, triads, rhythm, form and basic aural skills.

    Reflection: Music composition is the bane of my existence: one of the most difficult aspects of this class and classical music. This really surprised me, given my classical background in music. Music theory isn't too bad though.

Summer 2021

  • BMGT110: Introduction to the Business Value Chain (3)

    • Course Description: Students are provided with an introduction to the business value chain with an emphasis on inter-organizational and intra-organizational coordination of core business processes. Emphasis is on cross-functional integration and the efficient and effective management of core processes with an emphasis on marketing, operations and supply chain management.

    Reflection: I felt like I was familiar with a lot of the material to begin with. From AP Macroeconomics with Kachur and my FBLA projects, the first half of the class was full of content I was mostly familiar with. THe second part includes financial, accounting, and promotions topics--topics I'm not familiar with.

Fall 2021

  • CMSC216: Introduction to Computer Systems (4)

    • Course Description: Introduction to the interaction between user programs and the operating system/hardware. Major topics include C programming, introductory systems programming, and assembly language. Other concepts covered include UNIX, machine data representation, thread management, optimization, and virtual memory. Programming is done in the Linux Environment.

    Reflection: So far the most difficult class I've taken at UMD. I know so many people blame the instructor for this one, but I think we all could have studied harder. Subtle flex, but I've had a 4.0 for 8 years. And this is the first class that I'm getting a B in, post-curve. Pre-curve, I have a 65.something and the average is a 63.something. I could go on and on about how it was a different professor making everything more difficult and unusual as people did on rate my professor, but I won't. Genuinly just a difficult class that is quite different from all the other CS classes so far and it requires different attention. I'm really hoping I learned from this experience and change my approach to the rest of the semesters.

  • HNUH268Y: The Politics of Disability: Life Narratives & Identity (3)

    • Course Description: The politics of disability are fraught and contentious. This course takes up the experience of disability by asking how and why differently-abled bodies are excluded, marginalized, or threatened. Students will examine these questions through the psychosocial and cultural history of disability, as well as through life narratives and real-world exploration. Beginning with the history of disability, students will gain an understanding of how current disability identity and culture has develop in the US. Students will experience and also produce personal life narratives related to bodily politics and gain insights that help them navigate the politics and participate in the change-making advocacy of disability.

    Reflection: I feel like I've been pretty lucky with the honors classes I've chosen. All of the classes I've chosen have been entirely reasonable with workload and expectations. I know some of the other classes are so much harder and so much heavier in workload and extremely high grading expectations. I feel like this class was so great and educated me so much on disability. I also had a super fun time working on the final project.

  • INST123: Databases for All (3)

    • Course Description: An introduction to relational databases for students with no previous programming experience. Provides a means for students of diverse backgrounds to successfully learn how to store, retrieve, and maintain data in relational databases. Topics include a brief comparison of database systems with an emphasis on relational databases, fundamental relational database concepts, and data types. Includes technical approaches to accessing information stored in relational databases.

    Reflection: Great introduction to SQL. To be honest, I was wary about taking this class because I had dropped ENES100 for this and wasn't sure about the workload. Thankfully, it wasn't bad at all. The workload was entirely manageable and there wasn't a final exam at the end, which saved me studying time since I had two finals already. The group project honestly was kinda nice because I got to meet some other people, but it wasn't something that you could blow off.

  • STAT400: Applied Probability and Statistics I (3)

    • Course Description: Random variables, standard distributions, moments, law of large numbers and central limit theorem. Sampling methods, estimation of parameters, testing of hypotheses.

    Reflection: One of the most enjoyable classes. I think it was the way that the class was structured, but it didn't seem too difficult. The numerous examples provided definitely helped with my success in the class. Mazzullo is the best!

Spring 2022

  • CMSC330: Organization of Programming Languages

    • Course Description: A study of programming languages, including their syntax, semantics, and implementation. Several different models of languages are discussed, including dynamic, scripting (e.g., Ruby, Python) functional (e.g., OCaml, Haskell, Scheme), and memory safe systems programming (e.g., Rust). Explores language features such as formal syntax, scoping and binding of variables, higher-order programming, typing, and type polymorphism. Introduces finite automata, context free grammar, parsing, lambda calculus, and basics of security attacks and software security.

    Reflection: All I took away was that I don't like this class.

  • CMSC351: Algorithms

    • Course Description: A systematic study of the complexity of some elementary algorithms related to sorting, graphs and trees, and combinatorics. Algorithms are analyzed using mathematical techniques to solve recurrences and summations.

    Reflection: This was much more interesting than CMSC330, and I definitely think that this class is incredibly beneficial for future software engineering interviews. A lot of the things we learned in this class, including sorting methods and time complexity are things we need to know as software engineers. Super important and interesting class.

  • HNUH268A: Arbitrating our Bodily Rights: What it Means to Consent

    • Course Description: Both American law and conventional morality support the view that human beings have bodily rights - ones we can waive by giving another person our consent to do what would otherwise be a rights violation. Yet, debate about the sovereignty of an individual's body and the potential rights of others--including the state--over our bodies lies at the core of some of the most contentious issues facing us as a society. This class will investigate the thorny debates that pertain to consent in sexual relations, medicine and research, and bodily markets. What types threats, offers, or cognitive incapacities undermine true consent? How do we evaluate the moral or legal significance of bodily rights when they come into tension with the government's interest? After evaluating arguments, exploring distinctions, and using philosophical tools to arrive at reasoned conclusions about consent, students will learn to apply these conceptual tools to cases from fiction and current events.

    Reflection: This class was also another interesting and challenging class. I didn't really know anything, but I learned a lot by the end of this course. The course also gave me a lot more awareness to things happening with rights and consent, and I really enjoyed this class despite it being difficult for me.

  • HNUH300: Vantage Point Seminar

    • Course Description: Goal-setting and project-design seminar required of all UH students and taken in the second semester of the sophomore year or the first semester of the junior year.

    Reflection: A great way to end University Honors--took the class with my friends and met a few new people who are also interesting.

  • MUSC210: The Impact of Music on Life

    • Course Description: Music as a part of culture. Materials drawn from traditions throughout the globe to illustrate issues of historical and contemporary significance, including the impact of race, class and gender on the study of music.

    Reflection: I wish there was more emphasis on classical music, but other than that, it was a fine class and typical for gen eds.
